Reading the Chart — Plain Language Guide

What is the sawtooth shape?

Each rising diagonal line represents the fault silently building up stress underground. The tectonic plates are pushing against each other continuously — like slowly pressing down on a spring. When the spring can no longer hold, it releases. That release is the earthquake. The line drops vertically to zero, and the cycle begins again.

Why do some peaks reach higher than others?

A higher peak means the modeled phase interval lasted longer before an observed release or watch outcome. It does not guarantee stronger shaking by itself. Strength is logged separately after observation as micro-pulse, small shake, significant felt shake, clustered activity, delayed release, or no confirmed local release.

What are the multipliers (1×, 3.5×, 7×, 9.5×)?

The model uses a natural “heartbeat” of about 35.5 minutes to mark watch centers. The 1×, 3.5×, 7×, and 9.5× values are phase markers. They help organize timing, while observed amplitude and official confirmation remain separate fields.

What is the “Observer vs Instrument” line at the bottom?

Two timelines are overlaid: the observer’s notes (manually recorded times) and the instrument catalog (official seismograph records). The small gap between them is “clock drift” — a tiny timing difference between human observation and machine timestamps. Both lines agree closely, which validates that the pattern is real, not an artifact of imprecise recording.
