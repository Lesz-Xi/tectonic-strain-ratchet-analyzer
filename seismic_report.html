▲ Top Chart — “The Stress Buildup”

Think of this like a rubber band being slowly stretched. The rising line shows stress building up silently underground. When it reaches a breaking point, the fault slips — that’s the sudden drop you see on the chart. Then the cycle restarts.

Blue line (Observer Notes) — What you personally felt and recorded. These are ground-truth observations from the field.
Orange line (Instrument Catalog) — What seismometers officially recorded. Sometimes slightly different due to sensor lag or location.
Dashed lines — The model’s predicted stress thresholds. When the fault reaches one of these, it releases energy (aftershock).
×9.5 Multiplier labels (1×, 3.5×, 7×, 9.5×) — How many “rhythm beats” of ~35 minutes the fault waited before releasing. Bigger number = longer buildup = bigger potential release.
▼ Bottom Chart — “Did the Clocks Match?”

This chart compares when you felt it vs when machines recorded it. The connecting lines between the two rows show how closely they match. A short line = great agreement. A long line = the observer and instrument disagreed on the timing.

Top row (Observer) — Each dot is a shock you felt. Plotted on a timeline showing minutes since the mainshock.
Bottom row (Instrument) — Each dot is a shock the seismometer recorded. If it lines up with yours — perfect match.
+3m Drift labels (+Xm / −Xm) — How many minutes apart the two readings were. “+3m” means the instrument was 3 minutes later than you felt it.
✓  Verification passes when drift is under 15 minutes — meaning your observations and the official catalog are telling the same story.
💡 The Big Picture — What This All Means

The Cotabato fault is behaving like a metronome. Every ~35 minutes, stress accumulates to a threshold. When it crosses that threshold — multiplied by 1×, 3.5×, 7×, or 9.5× — the ground releases energy as an aftershock. The fact that both the observer notes and the instrument catalog show the same rhythm independently is strong evidence that this is a real, predictable stick-slip pattern — not random noise.
